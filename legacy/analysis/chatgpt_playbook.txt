1. Winning‐solution “playbook” (noisy,
imbalanced text tasks)
Kaggle/DACON winners universally rely on ensembles and fine-grained calibration to squeeze out the
last points. For example, one first‐place Kaggle solution reported adding a Bi-LSTM head on top of BERT
and using Adversarial Weight Perturbation (AWP), which gave a CV rise from 0.858→0.861 (+0.003)
【39†L237-L239】. Similarly, model ensembling is standard: competitors often average 10–20 models
(or more) via weighted averaging or Weighted Box Fusion. Kaggle writeups report up to 19–38 models
ensembling for 1–3% F1 gain【100†L232-L240】【101†L218-L226】. Simple “one-model” entries
usually place far below finalists. Per-class calibration (threshold or logit bias tuning) is also widely used:
adding small class-dependent biases typically nets another few 0.001–0.005 F1【39†L229-L235】. In
short, the last 3–8% generally comes from very heavy ensembles (multi-seed, multi-fold, multi-
architecture) and post-hoc threshold optimization. For instance, one solution used a 19-model weighted
ensemble and a 10-model WBF ensemble to lift F1, and many teams report +0.005–0.01 gain from
adversarial training/weight perturbation【39†L229-L239】【100†L232-L240】. No single trick gives
+0.05 by itself (outside pseudo-labeling), but combined they can move the needle by a few points.
2. Compact models vs. XLM-R/DeBERTa under
1GB
Recent research suggests new encoders and LLM-based classifiers can match or beat XLM-R/DeBERTa
at modest sizes. Notably, ModernBERT-base (149M params) achieves SOTA on GLUE benchmarks and is
~2–4× faster than BERT/RoBERTa【45†L39-L44】【48†L662-L670】. ModernBERT-base even surpasses
DeBERTaV3-base on GLUE【48†L662-L670】, implying it should at least tie XLM-R-base on classification.
Its smaller size (139M vs. XLM-R’s 278M) also makes it a safe fit (<1GB). We expect a ModernBERT model to
yield a modest lift (≈+0.01–0.02 F1) in place of XLM-R, with similar or faster inference (fits GPU easily).
LoRA‐tuned causal LLMs are another promising avenue. A recent study fine-tuned small multi-lingual
decoders (Qwen 0.6B–1.5B) with LoRA adapters and a linear classification head. The embedding-based
approach (freezing most weights) matched or exceeded BERT performance on classification【49†L72-
L81】. E.g. a 0.6B Qwen3 model achieved F1≈0.828 on a single-label task【51†L7-L10】, rivaling XLM-R
(≈0.80). (Larger Qwen2.5/1.5B gave ~0.823–0.828【51†L1-L4】.) A 0.5–0.6B Qwen model in 4-bit
precision plus ~40M LoRA parameters could fit under 1GB and run on a T4, yielding a competitive
classifier. Thus small LLM + LoRA is worth a try, though full 1.5B models likely exceed 1GB.
Embedding models like GTE or E5 (multilingual) provide another path. GTE-base (110M) “outperforms
OpenAI’s large embedding model”【54†L33-L42】. One could freeze GTE/E5 embeddings and train a
small downstream head. In principle, this approach is very fast (just an MLP on top) and well within size
limits. However, no published results show these beat fine-tuned transformers on classification. We
expect similar or slightly worse F1 than XLM-R, but they can serve as efficient baselines.
Finally, Korean-specialized encoders (e.g. KoBERT, KLUE-BERT, KoELECTRA, KcBERT, etc.) could
marginally improve understanding of Korean text. These models (110–120M) fit well, but in practice top
Kaggle teams stick to multilingual or mixed models. Given our data is code-mixed, a Korean model alone

3. Noise-robust training (confusable classes)
For classes with irreducible label noise, robust loss functions and training schemes can help preserve
macro-F1. The Bi-Tempered Logistic Loss [Amid & Warmuth 2019] is specially designed for this: it
“introduces bounded loss and heavy-tailed softmax” to resist noisy labels【74†L268-L277】【71†L173-
L181】. In practice it significantly improves accuracy under label noise (e.g. from 97.6→98.6% on MNIST,
and from 53.2→62.6% on CIFAR100)【74†L241-L250】. We expect a few percent gain on noisy text
classes. Similarly, Generalized/Symmetric Cross-Entropy losses (Zhang & Sabuncu 2018; Wang et al.
2019) interpolate between MAE and CE to temper the effect of outliers【78†L29-L38】【83†L29-L38】.
These have shown empirical robustness and could be simply implemented.
Other strategies: co-teaching (train two nets and filter noisy samples) can help if label noise is extreme,
but adds complexity. Label smoothing provides minor regularization (usually <1% gain). Directly
optimizing F1 (e.g. a soft‐F1 loss) is theoretically attractive but tricky; instead, we rely on usual CE-based
training plus post-hoc thresholding. Logit adjustment for class imbalance (Menon et al.) can correct bias
by modifying logits by class prior ratios; this may boost rare-class recall, but effects on macro-F1 are
usually modest unless priors are extreme.
In practice, bounded/tempered losses rank highest: e.g. Bi-Tempered often yields noticeable
improvements under noise【74†L241-L250】. GCE/Symmetric CE come next. Co-teaching and label
smoothing give smaller, less consistent gains. Tuning the CE temperature (making it “softer”) can also
help slightly. We will likely try Bi-Tempered and/or Symmetric CE with temperatures tuned, as these have
credible evidence of boosting F1 in noisy settings.
4. Data augmentation & regularization (train-time
only)
Under offline evaluation, training-time regularization must be used (no test-set tricks). R-Dropout
(consistency between dropout samples【93†L25-L33】) is reported to yield substantial gains: for
example, NeurIPS’21 R-Drop paper found “substantial improvements” on fine-tuning large models
(RoBERTa-large, etc.)【93†L25-L33】. In practice, R-Drop is easy to implement (double forward + KL loss)
and likely to give a small F1 bump (~1–2%).
Adversarial training (FGM/AWP) is also popular. Our own AWP trial gave +0.005–0.007 F1. Kaggle
solutions confirm AWP yields small but consistent boosts (one reported +0.01 CV【100†L283-L289】).
FGM (single-step embedding noise) is cheaper and may help similarly. AWP (perturb weights) gave up to
+0.002–0.01 on text tasks【100†L283-L289】, at higher compute cost. These are worth trying given the
gain/effort ratio.
EDA/back-translation augmentations often help smaller datasets【95†L29-L38】, but our data is
moderately large (70k) and code-mixed. Synonym replacement in Korean/English may help a bit, but
gains are likely <0.5% and implementing good Korean augmentation is tricky. Back-translation (KR⇆EN)
might introduce noise (esp. code terms). Mixup/Manifold-Mixup for text is less proven: some papers
suggest benefits in low-data regimes【90†L1-L7】, but for short code-mixed text it is uncertain. Overall,

5. Calibration and ensembling
Threshold/logit tuning: Because metric is macro-F1, the decision threshold per class matters. A
common Kaggle trick is to “tune F1 thresholds” on validation or adjust output biases. In our case, we
already saw +0.005 F1 from logit‐bias shifts per class. Further refining by optimizing each class’s cutoff
(or using e.g. coordinate ascent on F1) can squeeze a bit more (usually <+0.01 total). Menon’s logit-
adjustment (theoretically optimal under known priors) could be attempted, but simpler grid-search on
OOF is fine.
Ensembling: Multi-seed and multi-model ensembling often yields the largest gains (1–3%). Kagglers
average many runs: e.g. some solutions report up to 38 models ensembled【101†L218-L226】. Even a 3-
seed ensemble of XLM-R or ModernBERT can give ~+0.5–1% F1 by reducing variance. Stronger is to add
diverse models: e.g. ensemble XLM-R-base, ModernBERT, a small Korean model, etc. Two known
ensembling strategies are (a) simple weighted average of softmax outputs and (b) Weighted Box Fusion
(treating outputs like bounding boxes)【100†L232-L240】. Stacking (learning a meta‐model) has also
been used (e.g. linear regression on OOF preds)【100†L232-L240】【101†L218-L226】. In practice,
even a flat average of 5–10 strong models can add ~+1–2% macro-F1.
Given constraints (1GB offline), we must limit ensemble size. Multi-seed (same model, different seeds) is
cheapest – we can do 3–5 seeds easily. Multi-model (e.g. one ModernBERT, one XLM-R, one Korean BERT)
adds robustness but each model costs memory. A final option is distillation: train a single smaller model
on pseudo-labels generated by the ensemble. This “knowledge distillation” can capture ensemble gains
in one model for inference, though distillation itself may be complex. It’s worth considering if the
ensemble approach is too heavy; indeed, some top teams have distilled multi-model ensembles into one
final network.

7. Missed techniques and step-changes
We must emphasize what’s not viable here. Notably, test-set pseudo-labeling or transductive tricks are
disallowed, which in some competitions gave multi-point boosts. Semi-supervised methods (e.g.
pseudo‐labels on unlabeled data) can yield large gains【100†L327-L335】 but are blocked since the
test is hidden. Knowledge distillation from an ensemble to a single model is a possible way to “squeeze”
ensemble gains into one model – we flagged it via ensembling, but it might deserve a strategy note if
ensemble F1 is high but we need a single model.
No other radical “step-change” (like a completely new paradigm) is evident that we’ve missed. Methods
like multi-task learning or chain-of-thought prompting don’t apply. All major winning tactics (ensembles,
adv training, robust loss) have been covered.
8. Verdict and highest-leverage path
Reaching ~0.776 macro-F1 appears extremely ambitious given the noted noise ceiling (~0.5 F1 on a large
subset). Top-12 score is ~0.776, so far above our 0.690. Achieving it offline without test leakage likely
requires maximal ensembling and all of the above gains. In practice, pushing from 0.69 to 0.776 is a >10%
relative jump. Our noise analysis suggests some 40% of examples are inherently ambiguous; unless that
noise was unevenly distributed, even a perfect model might not exceed ~0.80.
Thus 0.776 may be at or slightly beyond the noise limit. The highest-leverage path is an extensive
ensemble of carefully fine-tuned models (using R-Drop, AWP, robust loss, etc.), with tuned thresholds and
possibly distilled into a final model. In other words, combine as many of the above gains as possible. If
we must pick one single path, it’s aggressive ensembling of diverse, robustly-trained encoders: for
example, fine-tune ModernBERT-base, XLM-R-large, a LoRA-Qwen, and a Korean BERT (each with R-
Drop+AWP+tempered loss), then average them. This wide net has the best chance to hit ~0.776. But be
warned: the improvements beyond ~0.75 F1 may be marginal given noise. Every percentage point past
~0.75 will be very hard.
In summary: focus on scalable, proven techniques – new compact encoders (ModernBERT or LoRA
LLM), robust loss, consistency/adv training, then exploit ensembles and threshold tuning. These are the
concrete strategies with evidence of real gains【39†L237-L239】【100†L232-L240】【93†L25-L33】
【74†L241-L250】. With them combined, one may approach the high 0.70’s F1, but breaking 0.776 will
likely require miraculous label consistency or unlabeled data tricks (not allowed). Handle the ambiguous
classes as best as possible, but prioritize moving all classes up consistently.
